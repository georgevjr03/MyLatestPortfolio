// Aviation Passion
The only company that could make me more excited about jet engines is the one that builds them.
I have followed GE Aerospace's engine programmes closely for years — not as a career strategy, but because I find the engineering genuinely extraordinary. The thermodynamic cycles, the materials science behind thermal barrier coatings, the mechanical elegance of a multi-stage compressor — these are things I read about because I want to, not because someone assigned them.

I followed the GE9X certification programme for the Boeing 777X milestone by milestone. I understand why the composite fan blades represent a step-change in efficiency. I have read about the LEAP-1A development cycle and the challenge of achieving the bypass ratio targets within an existing nacelle envelope. The GEnx powering the 787 Dreamliner is a personal favourite — the architecture that proved composite aerostructures and high-bypass turbofans could redefine long-haul economics at the same time.

The ECT programme at GE Aerospace is not a job application for me. It is the most direct path I can imagine to learning this field from the inside.
GE9X
Boeing 777X · Programme I follow most closely
Composite fan blades, highest bypass ratio in GE history, 3D-woven CMC turbine components. The engine I have tracked from first test run to certification. A generational achievement in propulsion.
LEAP-1A/B
A320neo · 737 MAX
CFM's answer to next-generation narrowbody efficiency. Twin-spool, high-bypass, advanced 3D aerodynamic blades. Powers the aircraft that dominate short-haul operations in every market.
GEnx
Boeing 787 Dreamliner
The engine that validated composite aerostructures and high-bypass turbofan performance together. The 787's economics would not exist without the GEnx family's specific fuel consumption targets.
